The golden eagle (Aquila chrysaetos) is a bird of prey living in the Northern Hemisphere. It is the most widely distributed species of eagle.

One of the largest, fastest, nimblest raptors in North America

Appearance

Golden eagles are large birds with a wingspan of up to 86.6 in (220 cm) and a length of 27.6-33.1 in (70-84 cm). They have a dark brown body with a golden sheen on their neck and head.

Habitat

Golden eagles live in open and semi-open areas of the Northern Hemisphere, such as mountains, canyonlands, and riverside cliffs. They avoid developed areas and forests.

Hunting

Golden eagles are powerful hunters that prey on small to medium-sized mammals, such as rabbits, hares, ground squirrels, and prairie dogs. They can also take down larger prey, such as deer, swans, and cranes.

Behavior

Golden eagles are solitary or pair-living birds that are highly territorial. They are aggressive towards other eagles and will escort them from their territory.

Breeding

Golden eagles typically lay two eggs, but the number can range from one to four. The incubation period lasts 35-45 days, and the eaglets start flying at about ten weeks old.

Lifespan

Golden eagles can live up to 32 years in the wild, and up to 46 years in captivity

Cultural Significance of Golden Eagle

The golden eagle holds deep cultural significance across various cultures. In Indigenous American traditions, eagles are sacred symbols of strength, courage, wisdom, respect, and honor, believed to have a divine connection and central to spiritual ceremonies. For the Aztecs, the golden eagle represented the Sun, served as a noble symbol and a messenger of the god Huitzilopochtli, and even functioned as currency for tribute payments. In Mongolia, eagle hunting is a revered tradition, with hunters showcasing their skills in competitions. Additionally, the golden eagle has a long history in falconry, honored in various ancient cultures. In the U.S., the Fish and Wildlife Service's National Eagle Repository supplies eagle feathers to Native Americans for religious and cultural practices.

Funny Thing About Golden Eagles

Golden eagles sometimes “play” with their prey mid-flight, showing off their strength and wild sense of fun!

Unique Thing About Golden Eagles

Golden eagles have remarkable eyesight that allows them to spot prey from over two miles away. Their vision is about eight times sharper than a human's, enabling them to detect even the slightest movement on the ground while soaring high above. This incredible eyesight, combined with speeds of up to 200 mph during a dive, makes them one of the most skilled hunters in the bird world.
